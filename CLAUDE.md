## 1. WHAT THIS PROJECT IS

The 19th Hole is a **private entrepreneur club** being built in Ogden, Utah. The founders — Josh Zieglowsky and Zach Spencer — are raising **$3.9 million** from **12 Founding Circle Partners** at **$325,000 per seat**. Every partner receives General Partner (not LP) status. The club is structured as an LLC.

This repository contains:
1. **`index.html`** — The full 18-slide investor pitch deck (browser-based, full-screen presentation)
2. **`editor.html`** — A "fix-it" editor page where Josh and Zach can make live content edits without a developer

The pitch deck is a single self-contained HTML file with both founders' family photos embedded as base64. It uses Google Fonts and arrow-key/touch navigation. It was designed to run in Chrome, full-screen (F11), for in-person investor meetings.

## 7. TAX ADVANTAGE

### Cost Segregation Strategy
The 19th Hole uses **accelerated cost segregation** — a legal IRS-recognized strategy that reclassifies building components (fixtures, finishes, equipment) to shorter depreciation schedules (5, 7, and 15 years) instead of the standard 39-year commercial schedule.

| Method | Year 1 Deduction |
|---|---|
| Accelerated cost segregation | **$975,000** |
| Standard 39-year schedule | $100,000 |

### The Math for a Founding Circle Partner
- Deductions available: $975,000
- Assumed tax bracket: 37% (target investor profile)
- **Estimated Year 1 tax savings: ~$360,000**
- Investment cost: $325,000
- **Net result: Tax savings alone exceed the full cost of the investment before the club opens**

This is a major selling point. The investment effectively pays for itself in Year 1 via tax benefits, before a single member walks through the door.

> ⚠️ Always caveat: these are estimates. Investors should consult their own tax professionals. TaxIQ (one of Josh's ventures) specializes in exactly this type of planning.

## 10. THE "WHY NOW / WHY US" FRAMEWORK

### FIRST — First-Mover in Northern Utah
No competitor has built this category in Northern Utah. First-mover advantage in a geography that is growing faster than almost anywhere in the US means owning the category before anyone else can stake a claim.

## 13. THE EDITOR PAGE (editor.html)

### Purpose
Josh and Zach should be able to sit down with a potential investor, hear feedback, and immediately update copy or numbers without calling a developer. The editor page gives them a clean interface to:

1. Edit any text content on any slide
2. Update financial numbers
3. Change contact information
4. Save changes that persist to `index.html`

### How to Build the Editor

The editor should be a separate `editor.html` page that:

**Loads the presentation data from a JSON config** (`content.json`) that drives both pages. This means:
- `index.html` reads `content.json` on load and renders slides from it
- `editor.html` shows a form UI for every editable field
- Saving in the editor writes back to `content.json`
- Both pages hot-reload when content changes

**OR** (simpler approach for Vercel static hosting):
- `editor.html` loads `index.html` in an iframe
- Overlays an editing sidebar with labeled fields matching slide content
- On "Save", generates an updated `index.html` and triggers a browser download
- Josh or Zach drag the downloaded file over to replace the current one

**Recommended editor UI:**
- Sidebar divided by slide number (slide 1, slide 2, etc.)
- Each slide section shows the editable text fields with clear labels
- Input types: text inputs for short strings, textareas for longer copy, number inputs for financials
- Live preview: changes reflect in the iframe in real time
- "Download Updated Deck" button at top
- Simple password protection: `password = "19thhole2026"` (ask Josh/Zach to change this)

### Fields That Should Be Editable

**Contact / Identity:**

## 15. CONTENT DECISIONS & RATIONALE

### Why "private entrepreneur club" not "co-working space"
Tested framing during development. "Co-working" kills the premium positioning. The target investor has tried WeWork or Regus and found it generic. "Private club" signals exclusivity, curation, and status — all things this investor cares about.

### Why single membership tier ($500/mo)
A tiered structure (Social/Executive/Corporate) adds complexity that dilutes the "you either belong here or you don't" message. For the Founding Circle pitch, a single tier signals confidence. Price differentiation can be introduced post-launch.

### Why "General Partner" not "investor"
Every Founding Circle partner is a GP. This is not passive capital — it's ownership. Using "investor" or "shareholder" undersells the relationship. GPs have skin in the game, a voice in decisions, and genuine ownership.

### Why the tax slide is crucial
For the target investor (high-income entrepreneur, likely in the 37% bracket), $360K in Year 1 tax savings on a $325K investment is the single most compelling financial argument. It means the investment costs them effectively zero in Year 1 after tax benefits — before the club generates a dollar of revenue. This slide often closes the deal in the room.

### Why the proof of concept slide exists
The biggest objection to this pitch is "has anyone done this before?" Club Paddock (opened August 2024 in Utah) proved the model breaks even in 90 days. Zach's S2 Pickleball proves the operators know how to build a community. The proof slide answers both objections before they're raised.

### Why no "200 members long-term" projection
An earlier version of the deck showed 200 members as the long-term target. This was removed because:
1. It implies the club scales to a crowded general co-working space
2. 80–100 members is a more credible and exclusive ceiling
3. The premium pricing ($500/mo) is defensible at 80–100; less so at 200
4. Quality over quantity is the brand promise

### Why the logo was changed
The original design used a clipart-style laurel wreath XIX badge (from the designer). This was replaced with a clean geometric XIX mark — square border with flanking gold lines — because it reads as architectural and refined rather than template-generated.

### Why HTML instead of PowerPoint
The PPTX was built first using pptxgenjs. The output looked poor — flat colors, no gradients, limited typography, no photo cropping. HTML with CSS allows: true dark gradients, Google Fonts, the exact gold palette, smooth transitions, and full photos. The presentation runs in Chrome fullscreen and looks indistinguishable from a custom-built web app.
